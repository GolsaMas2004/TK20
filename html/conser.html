Conservatism



Conservatism in the simplest terms means carrying on existing traditions
rather than changing them. To conserve is to keep and not give up.

There are really two types: the personal kind that resists change because
it’s uncomfortable, and the political kind that is represented
by an ideology.

The development of a Conservative ideology took place after
the English Commonwealth was overthrown in 1660 when the monarchy was restored.
The English Tory party developed a Conservative ideology as it worked to restore the old traditions.

In America, there was no Conservative ideology until after
World War II. It developed as the philosophical structure of the Republican Party
based on Libertarian ideals, traditionalism, and anti-communism.
This ideology reached its peak with the election of Ronald Reagan in 1980.

In the time before there was an operating Conservative ideology in the United States,
there was always a Conservative faction resisting opposition efforts to move the country
in one direction or the other. The oddest case of this has to be the Civil War which
pitted Conservatives against Conservatives.
The North and President Lincoln sought to conserve (keep) the union,
resisting the change that would tear it apart. The South sought to conserve
their traditional way of life, which included a plantation economy and slavery.
